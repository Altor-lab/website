Altor vs. Building In-House: The Real Cost Tradeoff for Production AI

May 24, 2026 · Altor · 8 min read

Direct Answer

If you need one production AI system this quarter, Altor is usually the lower-risk path: 3 weeks to first system, $35K to $90K upfront, and year-one total cost of roughly $45K to $110K. Building in-house makes more sense when AI is a long product roadmap, not a single workflow problem. The in-house route looks cheaper on paper because salary is already budgeted, but year-one cost is usually higher once you count senior AI time, 0.3 FTE maintenance, slower launch, and the risk of pilot failure. The tradeoff is speed and accountability versus long-term internal ownership.

This comparison is easier to understand if you separate two goals. One goal is to get a production AI workflow working now. The other is to build internal AI capability as a long-term function. Those goals are not the same, and teams often choose the wrong path because they treat them as identical.

If your problem is a repeated support investigation, billing diagnosis, or API debugging path, then time-to-production matters more than internal org design. If your goal is to make AI a permanent product capability across several teams, the math changes.

Why Altor is cheaper for a narrow production problem

Altor is built for one class of problem: a repeated workflow that already costs real operating time. That is why the engagement starts with existing systems and live data. The scope is narrow on purpose. You are not funding a research team. You are funding a production result.

That changes the economics. Security and permissions are handled from day one because the system has to touch production data immediately. Integration maintenance stays with Altor because that is part of the productized service. Most important, the delivery model starts from accountable deployment rather than a loose pilot that may never get hardened.

For a workflow like support investigation, that means you can connect the ticket system, logs, billing, bug tracker, and deploy history without asking your internal team to become an AI platform team first. Why in-house can still be the right choice

This is not a pure sales page because there are clear cases where building in-house is the better move.

Choose Altor when

1. You need a production result in weeks. If the workflow is already hurting support, engineering, or revenue operations, waiting two quarters for a team to form is usually the more expensive option.

2. The problem is one high-value workflow, not a long AI roadmap. If the work is concentrated in support diagnosis, billing investigation, or another narrow operating path, an external delivery model is efficient.

3. Your team needs accountability after launch. If the system failing in production creates customer pain, having the deployment partner remain responsible matters.

Choose in-house when

1. AI is core to the product itself. If multiple teams will build AI features over several years, internal capability compounds.

2. You already have spare senior AI and platform capacity. If the people, security process, and maintenance discipline are already in place, the marginal cost drops.

3. The system needs deep product coupling that changes weekly. When the AI logic is inseparable from the product roadmap, ownership inside the product team can be the right structure.

The real risk question: who carries failure

In-house work often looks safer because it keeps code inside the company. In practice, it can be riskier because pilot failure gets normalized. The system misses deadlines, the builder moves to another project, or the workflow never reaches dependable production use. The company keeps the code and loses the result.

With Altor, the scope is tighter and the accountability is externalized. That does not guarantee success, but it aligns incentives around one measurable outcome: the system has to run on live work. For teams that want a proof point fast, that alignment matters more than theoretical ownership of unfinished code.

For technical support, API issue diagnosis, and other repeated operations work, this is where the service model wins. For broad product AI, internal ownership often wins later. The question is not which path is more prestigious. It is which one gets the right system into production with the right owner.

Frequently Asked Questions

When should you hire an AI services company instead of building in-house?

Use an AI services company when you need a production system this quarter, the workflow is narrow and high value, and your internal team does not have spare senior AI and integration capacity. It is the better choice when speed and accountability matter more than owning a long roadmap from day one.

What does an AI services company cost?

For the comparison in this page, Altor projects typically range from $35,000 to $90,000 upfront, with a year-one total of about $45,000 to $110,000 depending on scope and ticket volume. That includes deployment and ongoing support within the engagement model.

How long does in-house AI development take?

A realistic in-house timeline for a first production AI system is 3 to 6 months, often longer if the team first needs to hire or reassign a senior AI engineer. That timeline includes integration work, evaluation, hardening, and operational ownership.

Who owns the AI system after deployment?

With Altor, Altor remains accountable for the deployed system and its playbook updates within the engagement. With an in-house build, your engineering team owns the system, the maintenance backlog, and the production incidents that follow.

How should you evaluate AI services firms?

Look for proof of production deployments, speed to first live workflow, read-only integration discipline, a clear maintenance model, and named responsibility when the system fails. The key question is not whether the firm can demo AI. It is whether it will stay accountable after launch.
